## A fifty-person design team, <em>AI-pilled.</em>

CarMax's design team had grown from ten people in 2018 to more than fifty by 2025. They were also, like every product organization at that moment, getting AI-pilled. Solutions were arriving before problems did: *we should build an intelligent vehicle matchmaker.* *We should build a proactive care companion.* Two of the example briefs the team brought us had, in fact, been generated by AI asking AI what AI should do.

The team's leadership saw the dynamic clearly enough to ask for help. They didn't want a tutorial on prompting, and they didn't want their designers retrained as AI engineers. They wanted to keep doing service design (for an omnichannel retailer with stores, a centralized call center, a website, and a brand the company actually cares about) while the most overhyped technology in a decade rewired every conversation in the building.

The brief, as we framed it back: get the team comfortable with AI as a design material, and keep AI in service of value rather than the other way around.

<PullQuote attribution="Patrick Quattlebaum, engagement lead">
The AI hammer is in full effect at CarMax. So how do we not just focus on AI, but really bring it back to what CarMax wants to position in the market as a brand, and to the people, internally and externally? AI in service of value, versus everyone in service of AI.
</PullQuote>

## The reframe: <em>a material, not a feature.</em>

The move that organized everything else was to stop treating AI as a feature category and start treating it as a design material. Henry Dreyfuss's line from the 1950s gave us the frame: *fit the new machines and materials to people, not people to the materials of the machines*. Wood has grain. Glass has tolerances. Mobile had its own grammar a decade ago that designers eventually internalized. AI is the next material. It will end up woven into nearly every service we touch, sometimes front stage, sometimes back, sometimes connecting the two. Designers need a vocabulary for that, the same way they once needed a vocabulary for tap targets and motion.

Reframing AI from *thing we deploy* to *material we work with* is what made the rest of the workshop possible. It put designers back in their own discipline instead of in a fight with engineering's roadmap.

## Three frameworks, <em>and a point of view that outlasted the workshop.</em>

Inside that reframe, I built three frameworks and a body of secondary research the team would carry forward after we left.

### Six design principles for AI as a material

A short list of principles to hold in mind when designing *with* AI, regardless of whether the surface is a chatbot, an ambient interaction, or a backstage automation:

1. **Contextual appropriateness**: the right thing at the right moment, not just a capable thing at any moment.
2. **Transparency**: show reasoning, sources, and uncertainty; don't perform confidence the system doesn't have.
3. **Graceful degradation**: there is always a way for the AI to not work, or to be offline, without breaking the service.
4. **Continuous handoffs**: when a human takes over, they inherit the context; the customer never has to repeat themselves.
5. **Earned complexity**: autonomy and capability accrue as trust does, over months and years, not on day one.
6. **Adaptive control**: both the user and the operator can dial how much the AI is allowed to do without them.

These were written for designers, not for ML engineers. They're the AI analogue of the mobile design principles a generation of designers internalized in the early 2010s.

### Four dimensions of AI in a service

A model, still in development, for the variables a service designer is actually choosing between when AI shows up in a moment:

- **Actors**: who is participating? Customer, front-stage associate, back-stage associate, supplier; and increasingly, the customer's own AI agent, the company's customer-facing AI, the company's internal-only AI, and the supplier's AI.
- **Interface**: explicit (you're using a chatbot, a dashboard), ambient (the lot reads your plate before you walk in), or mixed.
- **Collaboration model**: sequential, parallel, independent, or orchestrated. How is the work divided over time between the human and the AI?
- **Control model**: how much autonomy does the AI have, and who governs it?

The point of the four dimensions is that the choices are independent. You can have a customer's own agent (actor) interacting ambiently (interface) in parallel with a human (collaboration) under tight human review (control). The combinations matter; the framework gives a team a way to talk about them without arguing past each other.

### A spectrum for how much the AI is allowed to do

The fourth dimension, control, needed its own scale. The military has a useful one in *human-in-the-loop / on-the-loop / out-of-the-loop*, but that language doesn't travel well to a retail service team. I translated it into five points designers and product managers could actually use:

- **Manual**: the human is doing all the work; AI isn't in the loop.
- **Assisted**: AI drafts; the human reviews every output before it goes out (a clinician approving an AI-drafted note).
- **Monitored**: AI runs continuously; a human samples and reviews (a pricing model with daily oversight).
- **Automated**: AI runs without regular review; only flagged anomalies escalate to a human.
- **Autonomous**: AI self-governs; humans only intervene on severe issues.

Two things this scale enables. First, it lets a team commit to a deliberate level rather than drifting upward by default. Second, it lets the level *change* across a journey (assisted at first, monitored once trust is earned, and so on), which is what *earned complexity* requires in practice.

<InsightCallout label="Stance">
The frameworks aren't there to make AI more sophisticated. They're there to make AI **designable**: to give a service designer the same agency over an AI material that they already have over a digital interface or a contact-center script.
</InsightCallout>

### A POV worth keeping

Alongside the workshop, I built Harmonic's first AI-in-services point of view as a living document: human-to-AI interaction patterns, real-world use cases beyond chatbots (dynamic pricing, AR/VR, generative content, machine-learned personalization), emerging service-design methods being adapted for AI (drawn from *Touchpoint* and from interviews across my network), and a set of analogies useful when you have to explain what's actually new to someone who isn't a designer. It's not a finished artifact. It's a knowledge base the studio keeps building on. CarMax was the forcing function; the POV is the durable output.

## Two days, <em>built on paper on purpose.</em>

We led 60 to 65 percent of the agenda across two days with the full fifty-person team. The arc was deliberate: start from a real, AI-shaped solution someone had already proposed (the team picked *Proactive Care Companion*), then back the team out of the solution and forward through brand fit, value exchange, service moments, scenario sketching, and finally an adapted blueprint that split front-stage and back-stage into human and AI lanes. We made them work on paper. People left the room anyway and came back having vibe-coded a prototype in Figma; that was, in its way, also data.

## Both reactions <em>were the work.</em>

The framework I am most attached to didn't come from me. About halfway through, Patrick put up a slide that asked, in plain language: *what is the problem you are using this tool to solve?* Bottlenecks, information transfer, decision support, whatever it actually was. It cut through the room. I keep returning to it.

<PullQuote attribution="Whitney Masulis, reflecting on the workshop">
What is the problem that we're trying to fix here, and what is the right tool to use when we're trying to fix it? That was the slide. That's the discipline this whole thing is in service of.
</PullQuote>

The reactions split. Some designers left the room mid-session to go vibe-code in Figma. Others said, openly, that they didn't want to hear the word *AI* again. Both reactions were the work. The feedback we heard at the end was *unlock*: the anxiety in the room dropped, and the team had a vocabulary they could use the next time someone walked in with a solution looking for a problem.
