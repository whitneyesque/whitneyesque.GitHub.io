## In the first week, <em>five stakeholders asked for five different things.</em>

In the first week of the engagement I sat in on a stakeholder interview with the client's senior product leader. He was less than eight months into his role, sitting on top of a digital portfolio built over more than a decade and designed when the company was, in his words, "very device-centric." His new mandate was to figure out what that portfolio should become next. He told us a substantial body of provider research already existed; what he wanted from a new round of research wasn't more findings. He wanted a framework. Something consistent, scalable across six geographies, with shared language and governance, that he could use to coordinate decision-making across product teams that had been operating independently for years.

Two days later I sat in on the parallel interview with the client's CX leader. She was newly in role too, freshly moved from a product-aligned reporting structure to a horizontal one that looked across silos and audiences. She wanted the work to do something different. She wanted it to shift the conversation inside the organization, give CX a credible voice in strategic prioritization, and help engineering-DNA product teams sit in the problem space before defaulting to solutions. She was frank about the cultural challenge: her team often presented research and got eye rolls about why more research was needed. She explicitly asked us to celebrate using the existing research rather than always pushing for more.

By the end of the second week, the count was five stakeholders, each expecting something distinct from the same engagement. A product leader wanted a framework. A CX leader wanted a conversation tool. A research leader wanted methodology. Two adjacent function leads wanted the work to land in their roadmaps. A junior approach would have collapsed those into a single ask and built a single deliverable. The senior move — and the one that shaped everything I did over the next twelve weeks — was recognizing that the brief had not arrived yet, and that designing the research itself had to be the first act of stakeholder management.

## What I led, <em>and how I led it.</em>

I joined as service designer and research lead on a three-person core team. What I owned across the engagement:

- The end-to-end research approach across both stakeholder and provider research
- The interview guides — including a deliberate split between a guide for leaders and a more granular guide for the people who would actually use the artifact day-to-day
- All ten of the moderated 90-minute provider interviews
- Three iterations of the synthesis, including the two that didn't land
- The design implications that became the foundation for the opportunity workshop
- Research process management, weekly client recaps, and the recruiting recovery when our recruiting partner fell behind

The engagement lead held the strategic frame and stakeholder relationships, and pushed me hard on the synthesis until it landed. A senior service designer owned the visual journey artifact. An intern joined partway through. The team operated across three time zones — North America, Europe, and Australia. Most of the research design and execution sat with me.

## What the company was actually trying to become

The client is dominant in respiratory-therapy hardware. The growth thesis the leadership team is pursuing now is to generate revenue from digital services, not just devices. Adoption, retention, and workflow integration are the metrics that matter for the next chapter — and those require organizational capabilities a hardware company hasn't been built to operate. New decision-making rituals. New customer relationships. New ways of bringing research into product strategy. Both leaders I sat with told me the rituals for making decisions in the new structure did not yet exist.

<InsightCallout label="The framing that shaped the work">
  In a stable organization, a journey map is a research deliverable. In the organization the client was becoming, the map was being asked to function as decision-making infrastructure for an org that didn't yet have any.

  That distinction is the reason this engagement was a senior service-design problem rather than a research project. It also told me where the risks were: research fatigue, engineering DNA defaulting to solutions, a deliverable that gets presented once and shelved. The case for the work succeeding had to be made structurally — in how the research was designed and how the synthesis was framed — not in the rigor of the interviews alone.
</InsightCallout>

## Designing the research as two stages

I designed the research in two stages, intentionally separated.

**Stage one was stakeholder research.** I treated stakeholder interviews as a research method in their own right rather than as project intake. The deliverable would be used by people whose intent I needed to understand at the same level of rigor I would apply to any other user. The leader guide focused on intent: strategic drivers, team dynamics, future vision, current journey-mapping practice. The map-user guide — for the people on product, design, and research teams who would actually use the artifact — was about practice. How do journey artifacts get produced today? In what formats? With what shared language?

The leader interviews surfaced the dual brief, the five-stakeholder count, and a series of organizational risks I would not otherwise have read in time: a pattern of research fatigue, an engineering culture that resisted sitting in the problem space, a recent acquisition that signaled the portfolio was being rebuilt from the foundation up. The map-user interviews told me what would have to be true for the new artifact to be adopted rather than archived.

**Stage two was generative provider research.** Ten 90-minute remote interviews in June and July: three home medical equipment respiratory therapists and seven board-certified sleep-specialist physicians. I moderated all ten. Each interview walked the participant through three pieces — a get-to-know-you, an ecosystem-mapping exercise, and a step-by-step walkthrough of the two phases of patient care in scope. Consistent enough across interviews to enable cross-interview synthesis, open-ended enough to let the participant lead me into territory I had not anticipated.

## The recruiting problem, named honestly

The research did not run cleanly. The client had committed to handling participant recruiting and underestimated how long it would take. By week four I could see the timeline drifting. I raised it but did not push hard enough. By the mid-point retro, the team named it directly: sometimes we need to push back harder when we know something is going to fail. I caught it then, we adjusted, and we recovered enough to run the research, but we ended up with three respiratory therapists rather than the larger sample I had wanted. The final deliverable carries an explicit recommendation that further research validate and deepen the HME archetype.

Including that caveat in the final deliverable was uncomfortable. It was the right call. The case study is honest because the work was honest, and a senior research lead who would not name a sample limitation is a research lead the organization cannot trust on the next engagement.

## What the research found

The two provider archetypes are living in two different versions of the same broken system, and the patient is sitting in the gap between them.

### The respiratory therapist: a dedicated guide shouldering emotional weight

The respiratory therapists we spoke with were clinical professionals who derived genuine meaning from helping patients overcome fear and start therapy. They were also working at for-profit companies whose patients regularly accused them of putting profit before care. The setup model was evolving fast. Full-service in-person setup appointments common during the pandemic were being phased out and replaced by drop-shipped equipment, self-service instructions, and a respiratory therapist functioning as remote tech support. One participant told us the most frustrating part of her job was that she could no longer put her hands on the equipment a patient was struggling with.

<PullQuote attribution="Home medical equipment respiratory therapist">
  After a long week of convincing people that it will be okay, your social meter is just a little gone.
</PullQuote>

Another spoke to the role's evolution in language that surprised us:

<PullQuote attribution="Home medical equipment respiratory therapist">
  Ten years ago, I would've never gone into this work because I didn't want to be a secretary.
</PullQuote>

A third surfaced the shape of the daily emotional load:

<PullQuote attribution="Home medical equipment respiratory therapist">
  Patients say: "You don't care about my health, about my sleep, about my wellbeing. All you care about is money." If you hear that time after time, it gets to be very frustrating.
</PullQuote>

### The sleep specialist: an artist forced to paint by numbers

The physicians described being boxed in by four pressures at once. **Time:** one told us his practice was being asked to see five to six hundred new patients a week and was "literally not able to take care of them all." **Insurance:** the four-hour-a-night, twenty-one-day compliance rule shaped clinical decisions whether or not the physician thought it was clinically right. **Technology:** an electronic medical record that did not integrate with the equipment provider, a separate compliance reporting platform, and a patient messaging tool one physician described as "the bane of our existence" and another called "free uncompensated work." **Management:** decisions made above them about which equipment providers they could refer to. They wanted to provide individualized care. The system was optimizing for throughput and reimbursement.

### The patient sits in the gap

The structural finding that mattered most for the client's strategy emerged from comparing the two archetypes. One sleep specialist named it cleanly: when the equipment provider fails the patient, the patient does not blame the equipment provider. The patient blames the physician who sent them there.

<PullQuote attribution="Board-certified sleep specialist">
  They look at the equipment provider as my extension. I'm the one who sent them there.
</PullQuote>

The prescribing physician owns the relationship and bears the reputational risk. The equipment provider runs the day-to-day experience. The patient sees one continuous service. No actor in the system has authority over the whole thing. This is the structural gap a connected-health platform is positioned to occupy, and it became the through-line of the workshop two months later.

## Synthesis as craft

The synthesis was the part of this engagement where I learned the most, and where my work failed before it succeeded.

My first version of the insights deck organized the findings around the structure of the interviews: what we heard from respiratory therapists, what we heard from physicians, what cross-cut both. It was comprehensive. It was also unusable. It gave the team everything we had heard without giving them a way to act on any of it.

My engagement lead pushed back. The second version tried to fix the structural problem by adding a layer of synthesis on top of the interview findings. It was better. It still was not right. The synthesis layer was descriptive rather than directional. It told the team what was true. It did not tell them where to look.

The third version reorganized everything around design implications. For the respiratory therapists: *human touch* (operations were optimizing for cost, but referrals depended on human-quality experiences) and *patient misconceptions* (RTs were exhausted from working against age-old patient anxieties about therapy). For the physicians: *boxed in* (clinical autonomy under siege from outside forces), *time pressure* (rising patient volumes, messaging tools as unpaid labor), *information up front* (delays caused by missing context that better data could surface), and *trust in equipment providers* (physicians needed confidence in who they were referring to). Each implication paired evidence from the interviews with a directional question: how can the company use its position to do something about this?

The third version was shorter than the first and did less. It was the version that worked.

<PullQuote>
  The first version was a research summary. The third version was a design brief. I had been treating those as the same thing. They are not.
</PullQuote>

The lesson I took: synthesis is a craft separate from research. The version that captures everything you heard is rarely the version that gives the team something to act on. An engagement lead pushing back is the work functioning correctly. I am planning for that revision cycle on subsequent projects rather than treating it as a setback.

## The opportunity workshop: <em>where the artifact became infrastructure</em>

The workshop was the moment the artifact stopped being a research deliverable and started being decision-making infrastructure.

We ran two sessions at the end of July with the client's product, CX, and research teams. Day one opened with a research presentation walking the team through the maps and the design implications, then moved into structured "How Might We" generation grounded in specific moments on the journey. Day two was synthesis and prioritization: participants voted across four lenses — most valuable to providers, quick wins, most complex challenges, greatest revenue potential — on the twenty most promising opportunities.

Five themes surfaced from the prioritized opportunities: patient communication (moving away from phone-based outreach), mask fit and selection (a moment of truth that drove cost and adherence), coordination and data consolidation (the broken handoff between physician and equipment provider), compliance and monitoring (the four-hour-a-night problem), and insurance and policy management (the prior-authorization burden).

The workshop did the job the CX leader had asked for in the stakeholder interview, before any provider research had been conducted. She had wanted Product and CX in a room arguing about customer problems together. The maps became the thing the room argued about. That outcome was not accidental. It was the function I had been designing toward since week one.

I built into the workshop a discussion segment on rituals and governance, explicitly framing how the maps could continue to live and evolve after the engagement ended. The product leader's framework concern got addressed there. The CX leader's culture-change concern got addressed in the workshop dynamics themselves. Both leaders walked out of the second day with what they had asked for, in different forms.

## What the client received, <em>and what they actually got</em>

The client received two experience maps with consistent visual language and a documented method for reading and updating them. A backlog of twenty prioritized opportunities. An insights deck for circulation. A workshop recap. A discussion structure for embedding journey artifacts into product-development rituals over time.

What the client actually got was a working session of their own product and CX teams in a room together, arguing about a shared artifact for two days. The artifact gave them something to argue about. The argument was what the CX leader had needed in the first week.

## Three things <em>that traveled with me out of this engagement.</em>

**Reading the business is design work.** The most important insight from this project did not come from the provider interviews. It came from holding five stakeholder briefs at once, inside an organization mid-transformation, and designing the research to honor that mess. Stakeholder navigation is not a soft skill adjacent to the design problem. It is the design problem.

**Synthesis is iterated, not produced.** I drafted two versions of the insights deck before I drafted the one that landed. The lesson was not that I should have been more careful upfront. The lesson was that the first version is rarely the one that earns its place, and that an engagement lead pushing back is the work functioning correctly. I plan for that revision cycle now.

**Push back earlier on what you can see coming.** The recruiting timeline was visible by week four. I raised it. I did not raise it loudly enough. I caught it at the mid-point and recovered, but the cost was a smaller HME sample and a caveat in the final deliverable. The cost of pushing earlier is always smaller than the cost of recovering later, and the cost of an honest caveat is smaller than the cost of overclaiming a finding the sample cannot support.

This engagement raised my standard for what good research-led service design looks like. The research had to be rigorous enough to hold up under stakeholder scrutiny in two languages: the language of design (does this build empathy?) and the language of business (does this point to where we should invest?). The synthesis had to be iterated until it gave the team something to act on. The artifact had to be designed to function inside the organization after delivery, not just to be presented and admired. None of those decisions were heroic. All of them are what senior research-led service design, done well, looks like.
