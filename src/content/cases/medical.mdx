## Two new-in-role leaders, <em>two different briefs.</em>

In the first week of the engagement, I sat in on a stakeholder interview with the client's senior product leader. He was less than eight months into his role. He had inherited a portfolio of digital products built over more than a decade, all of them designed when the company was, in his words, "very device-centric." His new mandate was to figure out what the digital portfolio should become next.

He told us a substantial body of provider research already existed, conducted a year or two earlier. He believed the team understood the late-stage patient experience well. What he wanted from a new round of research was something specific: not more findings, but a framework. Something consistent, scalable across six geographies, with shared language and governance, that he could use to coordinate decision-making across product teams that had been operating independently for years.

Two days later I sat in on the parallel interview with the client's CX leader. She was newly in role too. Her function had just been flattened from a product-aligned reporting structure to a horizontal one that looked across silos and audiences. She wanted the journey work to do something different from what the product leader was describing. She wanted it to shift the conversation inside the organization, to give CX a credible voice in strategic prioritization, to help engineering-DNA product teams sit in the problem space before defaulting to solutions. She was frank about the cultural challenge: her team often presented research and got eye rolls about why more research was needed, and she explicitly asked us to celebrate using the existing research rather than always pushing for more.

These were not the same brief. One leader wanted a scalable framework. The other wanted a conversation tool. A junior approach would have collapsed them into a single ask. The senior move was recognizing that the deliverable had to do both jobs, and that the research had to be designed with both audiences in mind from the beginning. That recognition shaped everything I did over the next twelve weeks.

## Service designer and research lead.

I designed the research approach across both stakeholder and provider research, developed the interview guides, moderated all ten provider interviews, drove synthesis through three revisions, and shaped the design implications that became the foundation for the opportunity workshop and the final deliverables. Our engagement lead held the strategic frame and stakeholder relationships, and pushed me hard on the synthesis until it landed. A senior service designer on the team owned the visual journey artifact and was, by team consensus, the synthesis virtuoso of the engagement. A separate track on a journey-management software platform ran in parallel, led by another senior team member. A design intern supported synthesis. The team operated across the United States, Europe, and Australia.

## What the company was <em>actually trying to become.</em>

It is hard to describe the business stakes of this engagement without naming the strategic shift the company was navigating. The client is a global leader in connected health, dominant in sleep-apnea therapy hardware. The company built that position over decades through engineering excellence and clinical evidence. The growth thesis the leadership team is now pursuing is to generate revenue from digital services and software, not just hardware. Adoption, retention, workflow integration, and recurring revenue are the metrics that matter for the next chapter.

This is not a minor strategic adjustment. A device company optimizes for clinical performance and regulatory compliance. A digital-services company optimizes for user behavior over time. The organizational capabilities required are different. The customer relationships are different. The decision-making infrastructure is different.

The product leader had been brought in to figure out the next-generation provider platform that would eventually replace the legacy one. The CX leader had been brought in to give the customer voice a credible seat at the strategy table. Both were navigating a recent reorganization. Both told me the rituals for making decisions in the new structure did not yet exist.

## Designing the research <em>as two stages.</em>

I designed the research in two stages, intentionally separated.

**Stage one was stakeholder research.** I treated the stakeholder interviews as a research method in their own right rather than as project intake. The deliverable would be used by people whose intent I needed to understand at the same level of rigor I would apply to any user. I helped develop a structured interview guide with consistent question architecture across leaders, covering strategic drivers, team dynamics, current journey-mapping practice, and future vision. We made a deliberate choice to design a separate, more granular guide for "map users": the people on product, design, and research teams who would actually use the artifact day-to-day. The leader guide was about intent. The map-user guide was about practice.

That separation paid off. The leader interviews surfaced the dual brief described above, plus a series of organizational risks I would not otherwise have read in time: a pattern of research fatigue, an engineering culture that resisted sitting in the problem space, and a recent acquisition for diagnostics that signaled the portfolio was being rebuilt from the foundation up. The map-user interviews surfaced a different set of constraints: how journey artifacts were currently produced (in fragments, by different teams, in different formats, with no shared language), and what would have to be true for the new artifact to be adopted rather than archived.

**Stage two was generative provider research.** We conducted ten 90-minute remote interviews in June and July: three home medical equipment (HME) respiratory therapists and seven board-certified sleep-specialist physicians. I moderated all ten. The guide I designed walked each participant through three pieces: a get-to-know-you to establish role and tenure; an ecosystem mapping exercise (who they work with, what tools they use, where the friction points are); and an in-depth, step-by-step walkthrough of the two phases in scope (the period from prescription through device setup, and the period from setup through ongoing adherence). Consistent enough across interviews to enable cross-interview synthesis, open-ended enough to let the participant lead me into territory I had not anticipated.

## The recruiting problem, <em>named honestly.</em>

The research did not run cleanly. The client had committed to handling participant recruiting and underestimated how long it would take. By week four, I could see the timeline drifting. I raised it but did not push hard enough. By the mid-point retro, the team named it directly: sometimes we need to push back harder when we know something is going to fail. I caught it then, we adjusted, and we recovered enough to run the research, but we ended up with three HMEs rather than the larger sample I had wanted. The final deliverable carries an explicit recommendation that further research validate and deepen the HME archetype.

Including that caveat in the final deliverable was uncomfortable. It was the right call. The case study is honest because the work was honest, and a senior research lead who would not name a sample limitation is a research lead the organization cannot trust on the next engagement. The lesson I took, and am applying on subsequent projects, is that the cost of pushing back early on a vendor risk is always smaller than the cost of recovering later.

## What the research found.

The two provider archetypes are living in two different versions of the same broken system, and the patient is sitting in the gap between them.

### The HME respiratory therapist: a dedicated guide shouldering emotional weight

The HMEs we spoke with were clinical professionals who derived genuine meaning from helping patients overcome fear and start therapy. They were also working at for-profit companies whose patients regularly accused them of putting profit before care. The setup model was evolving fast. Full-service in-person setup appointments common during the pandemic were being phased out and replaced by drop-shipped equipment, self-service instructions, and a respiratory therapist functioning as remote tech support. One participant told us the most frustrating part of her job was that she could no longer put her hands on the equipment a patient was struggling with.

<PullQuote attribution="Home medical equipment respiratory therapist">
After a week of convincing skeptical patients that therapy will be okay, your social meter is just a little gone.
</PullQuote>

### The sleep specialist: an artist forced to paint by numbers

The physicians described being boxed in by four pressures at once. **Time:** one told us his practice was being asked to see five to six hundred new patients a week and was "literally not able to take care of them all." **Insurance:** the four-hour-a-night, twenty-one-day compliance rule shaped clinical decisions whether or not the physician thought it was clinically right. **Technology:** an electronic medical record that did not integrate with the equipment provider, a separate compliance reporting platform, and a patient messaging tool one physician described as "the bane of our existence" and another called "free uncompensated work." **Management:** decisions made above them about which equipment providers they could refer to. They wanted to provide individualized care. The system was optimizing for throughput and reimbursement.

### The patient sits in the gap

The structural finding that mattered most for the client's strategy emerged from comparing the two archetypes. One sleep specialist named it cleanly: when the equipment provider fails the patient, the patient does not blame the equipment provider. The patient blames the physician who sent them there.

<PullQuote attribution="Board-certified sleep specialist">
When the equipment provider fails, the patient blames me. I'm the one who sent them there.
</PullQuote>

## Synthesis <em>as craft.</em>

The synthesis was the part of this engagement where I learned the most, and where my work failed before it succeeded.

My first version of the insights deck organized the findings around the structure of the interviews: what we heard from HMEs, what we heard from physicians, what cross-cut both. It was comprehensive. It was also unusable. It gave the team everything we had heard without giving them a way to act on any of it.

My engagement lead pushed back. The second version tried to fix the structural problem by adding a layer of synthesis on top of the interview findings. It was better. It still was not right. The synthesis layer was descriptive rather than directional. It told the team what was true. It did not tell them where to look.

The third version reorganized everything around design implications. For the HMEs: *human touch* (operations were optimizing for cost, but referrals depended on human-quality experiences) and *patient misconceptions* (RTs were exhausted from working against age-old patient anxieties about therapy). For the physicians: *boxed in* (clinical autonomy under siege from outside forces), *time pressure* (rising patient volumes, messaging tools as unpaid labor), *information up front* (delays caused by missing context that better data could surface), and *trust in equipment providers* (physicians needed confidence in who they were referring to). Each implication paired evidence from the interviews with a directional question: how can the company use its position to do something about this?

The third version was shorter than the first and did less. It was the version that worked.

<PullQuote>
The first version was a research summary. The third version was a design brief. I had been treating those as the same thing. They are not.
</PullQuote>

The lesson I took: synthesis is a craft separate from research. The version that captures everything you heard is rarely the version that gives the team something to act on. An engagement lead pushing back is the work functioning correctly. I am planning for that revision cycle on subsequent projects rather than treating it as a setback.

## The opportunity workshop: <em>where the artifact became infrastructure.</em>

The workshop was the moment the artifact stopped being a research deliverable and started being decision-making infrastructure.

We ran two sessions at the end of July with the client's product, CX, and research teams. Day one opened with a research presentation that walked the team through the maps and the design implications, then moved into structured "How Might We" generation grounded in specific moments on the journey. Day two was synthesis and prioritization: participants voted across four lenses (most valuable to providers, quick wins, most complex challenges, greatest revenue potential) on the twenty most promising opportunities.

Five themes surfaced from the prioritized opportunities: patient communication (moving away from phone-based outreach), mask fit and selection (a moment of truth that drove cost and adherence), coordination and data consolidation (the broken handoff between physician and equipment provider), compliance and monitoring (the four-hour-a-night problem), and insurance and policy management (the prior-authorization burden).

The workshop did the job the CX leader had asked for in the stakeholder interview, before any provider research had been conducted. She had wanted Product and CX in a room arguing about customer problems together. The maps became the thing the room argued about. That outcome was not accidental. It was the function I had been designing toward since week one.

I also built into the workshop a discussion segment on rituals and governance, explicitly framing how the maps could continue to live and evolve after the engagement ended. The product leader's framework concern got addressed there. The CX leader's culture-change concern got addressed in the workshop dynamics themselves. Both leaders walked out of the second day with what they had asked for, in different forms.

## What the client received, <em>and what they actually got.</em>

Two experience maps, one for each provider archetype, with consistent visual language and a documented method for reading and updating them. A backlog of twenty prioritized How Might We opportunities, organized by lens. An insights deck for circulation across product and CX teams. A workshop recap deck with deep dives on the top opportunities and supporting research evidence. A discussion structure for embedding journey artifacts into product-development rituals over time.

What the client actually got was a working session of their own product and CX teams in a room together arguing about a shared artifact for two days. The artifact gave them something to argue about. The argument is what the CX leader had needed.

## Reflection.

Three things I took from this engagement.

**Reading the business is design work.** The most important insight from this project did not come from the provider interviews. It came from holding the dual brief from two new-in-role leaders, inside an organization mid-transformation, and designing the research to honor both. Stakeholder navigation is not a soft skill adjacent to the design problem. It is the design problem.

**Synthesis is iterated, not produced.** I drafted two versions of the insights deck before I drafted the one that landed. The lesson was not that I should have been more careful upfront. The lesson was that the first version is rarely the version that earns its place, and that an engagement lead pushing back is the work functioning correctly. I am planning for that revision cycle now.

**Push back earlier on what you can see coming.** The recruiting timeline was visible by week four. I raised it. I did not raise it loudly enough. I caught it at the mid-point and recovered, but the cost was a smaller HME sample and a caveat in the final deliverable. The cost of pushing earlier is always smaller than the cost of recovering later, and the cost of an honest caveat is smaller than the cost of overclaiming a finding the sample cannot support.

This engagement raised my standard for what good research-led service design looks like. The research had to be rigorous enough to hold up under stakeholder scrutiny in two languages: the language of design (does this build empathy?) and the language of business (does this point to where we should invest?). The synthesis had to be iterated until it gave the team something to act on, not just something to learn. The artifact had to be designed to function inside the organization after delivery, not just to be presented and admired. None of those decisions were heroic. All of them are what senior service-design work, done well, looks like.
