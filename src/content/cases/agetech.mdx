## A concept had eight months of work behind it, <em>and a real decision point ahead.</em>

An innovation team inside a conservative Fortune 500 insurer had spent eight months developing a concept for an AI-powered caregiving service. The idea had legs. But a concept is not a product, and positive reactions in concept testing are not the same as signal from real users.

The team had reached a real decision point: was there enough evidence to justify investing in the early technology needed to run a beta? Answering that meant building something closer to the actual experience. We needed to know whether caregivers would engage with an AI, whether the interaction would feel useful instead of cold or overwhelming, and whether a forward-looking care forecast would mean anything to someone managing a parent's decline.

Prototyping a digital service is a solved problem. Wireframes, clickable prototypes, Wizard of Oz: the field has good tools for simulating something that hasn't been built yet. Prototyping an AI service is different. The medium is the message in a way that's hard to fake. A caregiver looking at a static mockup of an AI interface isn't learning whether they trust it, whether the tone lands, whether the questions feel invasive or genuinely useful. They're just looking at boxes on a screen.

For the concept to be testable, the prototype had to behave like the service. It had to ask questions, adapt to answers, handle emotional content gracefully, and produce output that felt credible enough to react to. Only then could we learn what was actually wrong with it.

## I joined eight months in. <em>Scope: build the thing, then learn from it.</em>

The service was aimed at employed family caregivers: people working full time while also managing the care of an aging parent. At its center was an AI that could onboard a caregiver through a series of questions about their situation, generate a personalized multi-year forecast of care needs and costs, and serve as an always-available caregiving coach.

I joined the project eight months in, after foundational research and concept testing were complete. My scope for this phase: design and build a prototype functional enough to generate real signal, then test it with caregivers and synthesize what we learned. In practice, that meant owning the full questionnaire architecture, the system prompt and behavioral rules that governed how the GPT interacted with users, a compassionate language framework, and the design and facilitation of two rounds of moderated testing with 12 caregivers, including a mid-study redesign of the entire flow between cohorts.

I didn't know I'd have to invent most of these things when I started. I was developing my own deliverables and methods as I experimented.

## Two intertwined problems: <em>what to ask, and how to ask it.</em>

To build it, I used the client's enterprise OpenAI account and a set of four specialized AI projects, each with a different expert persona: one focused on data science and questionnaire architecture, one on service design and testing protocols, one on geriatric care content and language, and one on competitive intelligence and business analysis. Rather than one long, increasingly muddled conversation thread, I could route specific questions to the right expertise at the right moment. The geriatric care persona caught things late in the build that I wished I'd had it review earlier. That's a lesson I'll carry forward.

The build had two intertwined problems.

**The first was what to ask.** The forecast hadn't been content-defined yet. The team worked together to scope what a forecast should include, then I turned to the data-science persona to build an example. I prompted it to behave as a senior data scientist, because I wasn't supposed to bother the actual data scientists at the company. I asked it: if 10,000 average Americans who meet our target audience signed up and each created a profile saying they were caring for an aging parent, what are the 20 most common combinations of comorbidities? Of all possible inputs, which would have the biggest impact on the forecast? What's the smallest number of questions that would still let the model produce something specific?

The answer was a tiered architecture: required fields every user would answer, high-value fields that would meaningfully sharpen the output, and enrichment fields for users willing to go deeper. Subsets of questions branched by major diagnosis. There were also questions about the caregiver themselves: communication style, support network, emotional state. Those answers informed how the chatbot would interact with them.

**The second was how to ask it.** These are not easy questions. Describing your parent's cognitive decline, your financial stress, what you're most afraid of: that's a lot to hand to an interface you just met. The GPT needed adaptive skip logic so it wouldn't re-ask what it already knew. It needed compassionate language rules so it never used words like "decline" or "impaired" or "vulnerable." It needed careful judgment about when to acknowledge an answer emotionally and when to simply move forward. Over-acknowledging after every response started to feel patronizing fast.

<InsightCallout label="Design principle">
Never say "decline," "impaired," or "vulnerable." Always lead with what the care receiver can do. The system prompt enforced the questionnaire architecture and the compassionate language framework simultaneously. These aren't prompt engineering decisions. They're service design decisions about how this service speaks to people in pain.
</InsightCallout>

The tone reviews caught the obvious problems. Testing caught subtler ones. One of the forecast intake questions asked what state the care recipient lives in, meaning geographic state, because care costs vary dramatically by location. A participant read it as emotional state. Was she anxious? Confused? Depressed? The question was unambiguous to us. It was genuinely confusing to someone in the middle of a caregiving situation where "what state is she in" is a question they ask themselves constantly, and it never means geography.

That reframed how I thought about language in this context. It's not enough to check whether words are clinically appropriate or emotionally sensitive. You also have to check whether they collide with the language caregivers already use to describe their daily experience.

## The first cohort showed us <em>we'd designed the experience backwards.</em>

We ran two cohorts in March 2026, 12 caregivers total, recruited through an online research platform. All were employed full-time and actively caring for an aging family member.

**The first cohort showed us we'd designed the experience backwards.** The original flow front-loaded data collection so the team could take outputs and manually load them into the wireframes. Participants answered questions one at a time. At the end, they were shown a profile for their loved one and a profile for themselves. The profiles fell flat. After spending all that time on deeply personal questions, they were getting back summaries of information they already knew. The forecasts came next, presented as a static wireframe with only one tab open, so participants saw a fraction of the information. Their reaction was tepid. A dashboard appeared after that, which they again read as a summary of what they'd told us. The feature they were most excited about came last: situational guidance, where they could ask the chatbot about their specific caregiving challenges. Most participants barely reached it.

<PullQuote attribution="Synthesis of Cohort 1 feedback, caregiver intake testing">
I just gave you all that information. What did I get for it?
</PullQuote>

Another session exposed a structural problem with the chat medium itself. During the Dispatcher mode, the GPT presented a numbered list of ten yes/no screening questions. The participant worked through them, the conversation continued, and a second numbered list appeared further down the thread. By that point, the first list had scrolled off screen. When the participant typed "1," they were responding to the second list, but the GPT interpreted it against the first and pulled "missed medications," a condition that didn't apply to their situation at all. The conversation veered off on a false premise.

This wasn't a user error. The GPT had the full conversation history in memory; the user didn't. They could only see what was on screen. When you stack sequential numbered lists in a scrolling chat window, the user loses context of what's above the fold, and the model has no way to know that. The conversational format has real structural limitations for complex, multi-step interactions. The prototype was surfacing problems not just with the content or the tone, but with the container itself.

The forecast revealed a harder design problem in a separate session: the line between useful projection and premature diagnosis. The executive sponsor tested the prototype using his own family's caregiving experience from several years earlier, working through it as the person he was at the time. His mother-in-law had been showing early signs of confusion around time and money, but the family hadn't known whether that was the beginning of cognitive decline or a temporary response to losing her husband. The forecast didn't make that distinction. It projected a five-year care trajectory that assumed progressive deterioration: escalating costs, increasing dependency, eventual facility care.

His reaction was immediate. *This would have freaked us out at the time. It felt like the tool was making a big assumption about a diagnosis.* He was right. The forecast had taken ambiguous early signals and mapped them onto a single trajectory without acknowledging that grief, stress, and medical conditions can look identical at this stage. For a tool designed to help people plan, that's a serious problem. **A projection that assumes the worst without naming what it doesn't know doesn't feel like guidance. It feels like a verdict.** The forecast needed to present ranges and scenarios rather than a single path, and to be explicit about the limits of its own confidence.

Then the session that changed how I thought about the whole design. One participant's parent was in the final stage of dementia, and probably had only months to live. I wasn't facilitating that session. I was acting as the Wizard of Oz behind the scenes. Mid-session, I sent a message to the facilitator: skip the rest of the questions, and don't show the forecast. A five-year financial projection isn't something you put in front of someone whose parent has months or weeks left. We shifted gears in real time.

That moment clarified something about the entire design. The tool needed to account for the full range of phases someone might be in when they first encountered it, including end-stages, and it needed to adapt quickly. The participant whose parent was dying wasn't an outlier to route around. The most important design problems lived at the edges of the user population, not in the middle.

**Between cohorts, I redesigned the flow entirely.**

Five things changed about what participants were asked and what they got back.

**Entry point.** Cohort 1 began with sequential questions to build a complete profile before any guidance arrived. Cohort 2 began with eight named modes — Dispatcher, Checkup, Planner, Money Map, Translator, Rehearsal Studio, Family Sync, Pressure Valve — and let the participant pick where to start, based on what felt most urgent to them.

**Depth of questioning.** Cohort 1 asked a long sequence of questions to establish a baseline before delivering anything. Cohort 2 used a ten-question rapid triage inside whichever mode the participant chose, and went deeper only if they wanted to.

**Artifact delivery.** Cohort 1 produced template libraries organized around a set of planning frameworks — Path 1, Path 2, Path 3 — that participants needed to understand before they could use them. Cohort 2 produced specific named tools delivered directly: a Care Coordination Hub, a Medication Control Sheet, an Appointment Tracker. No meta-system to learn first.

**Actionability.** Cohort 1 outputs were comprehensive and planning-oriented. Cohort 2 outputs were ranked next steps the participant could take that week. After one Cohort 2 participant's ten-question triage, the system's first recommendation was to put a wearable alert device in place within the week, before anything else.

**In-flow adaptation.** Cohort 1 restarted when scope changed. Cohort 2 recalibrated. In one Cohort 2 session, the participant's situation updated three times mid-conversation — a second fall, a death in the family, a medication change — and each update refined the priority list without restarting.

The trade-off was deliberate. Less elegant than a single adaptive flow, but it gave people a sense of scope and control that the "smarter" version hadn't.

**Strategic reframe:** Value has to come before effort. Cohort 1 told us this in the most direct way possible: if you ask people to give a lot before they get anything back, you will lose them, especially if they're already stretched thin. The reframe wasn't just a UX choice; it changed the product's architecture.

## Cohort 2: <em>they leaned in.</em>

The difference was immediate. Participants leaned in. In almost every session, we had to tell them we needed to move on because they kept wanting to ask the AI more questions. Several said they learned something they hadn't known before. Nobody asked what they were getting in exchange for sharing so much personal information. That last point mattered as much as anything else: the interaction felt worth it to them.

The most telling signal came from an unplanned session. The executive sponsor, the person who would ultimately decide whether the concept justified further investment, tested the prototype using his own family's real caregiving experience from several years earlier. He already knew how the story ended. He wasn't in crisis. But within minutes, he stopped evaluating and started using it. He moved through the Dispatcher, asked for a forecast, then started asking follow-up questions the team hadn't anticipated: what doctors to look for, what to do if his mother-in-law wouldn't let anyone into her appointments, whether the tool could handle situations beyond memory loss. The team had to tell him they needed to move on. He kept going.

<PullQuote attribution="Facilitator note, executive sponsor session">
We can't get him to stop.
</PullQuote>

That moment mattered because of who he was in the project. A recruited participant engaging deeply is a good sign. The person who controls whether the concept gets funded engaging with it as a real user, pulling from his own experience, unable to disengage even after the scheduled session was over: that's a different kind of evidence. He wasn't reacting to a concept. He was experiencing a product solving a problem he'd actually had.

## The client got the signal they needed. <em>The next phase is substantially larger.</em>

The testing gave them enough confidence in the concept's desirability and the interaction model to justify moving forward. The next phase is substantially larger: building the production AI, developing the real forecast model with a data science team, and running a two-month beta this fall that will include the AI, the forecast, human care guide calls, and task management features. That beta is a meaningful investment. The prototype is what made it defensible.

The beta will produce the post-launch evidence: adoption, retention, what caregivers actually do with the AI when it's available to them at home. That data isn't in yet. What the prototype produced was the case for collecting it.

## Four things <em>that traveled with me out of this project.</em>

**Prototype fidelity has to match what you're trying to learn.** If the service is AI-delivered, a static mockup won't tell you whether people trust it, engage with it, or find it useful. The prototype has to behave like the thing. That's more work, and it's the only way to get real signal.

**Value has to come before effort.** Asking people to give a lot before they get anything back will lose them, especially if they're already stretched thin. Watching it fail in real time with caregivers made the principle concrete in a way reading about it never could.

**Design for the edges from the start.** The participant whose parent was dying wasn't an outlier to route around. That situation exposed real gaps in the design's assumptions. The most important design problems live at the edges of the user population, not the middle.

**The AI is not the designer. We are.** I used AI tools extensively on this project, both to build the prototype and to support the build process itself. They were genuinely useful. But every judgment call about what the experience should feel like, what to sacrifice and what to protect, what the data from testing actually meant: that was mine. The tools don't make those calls. We do.
