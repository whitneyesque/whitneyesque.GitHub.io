## The brief we were given, <em>and the brief we took on.</em>

A global quick-service restaurant chain was losing sales and losing market share. They hired Fjord and Accenture to fix their loyalty program. The assumption inside the brief was that better points mechanics would bring customers back.

We didn't think that was the real problem. The team challenged the assignment and pushed the client to face issues that ran deeper than updating a points system. Loyalty programs are a downstream lever. If customers are choosing competitors, or choosing to bring lunch from home, no amount of points-tuning will pull them back. The work that mattered was upstream: understanding why people were defecting in the first place, and what the brand could credibly become.

That reframe is the case. Everything that followed was made possible by getting the question right at the start: the research, the synthesis, the co-design workshop, the concept distillation.

## My role

I was one of two interaction designers on the team and worked on the project for about three months. My contributions fell into three areas:

- **Field research.** I ran sessions with customers and employees in stores and at a market research facility.
- **Synthesis and journey mapping.** I worked with the visual designer to create a combined customer-and-employee journey, which became the central artifact we shared with the client at the workshop.
- **Workshop facilitation and recap.** I facilitated one of the breakout teams during the co-design workshop with the client. Afterward I edited a recap video of the day, a deliverable the team hadn't produced before.

## Expanding the competitive set <em>to include lunch from home.</em>

Before we went into the field, we challenged the questions we'd been given and ran a competitive analysis. The move that mattered there: we expanded the competitive set to include all potential lunch options, not just other QSR brands. That meant a customer's choice to bring lunch from home was treated as competition. So was skipping lunch. So was a grocery-store deli, a coffee-shop sandwich, a meal-prep container.

That sounds obvious in retrospect. It wasn't obvious in the brief. The original framing treated the chain's competitors as other QSR brands, which kept the conversation in a narrow lane. Once "bringing lunch from home" was in the set, the strategic question changed: not "how do we win against the chain across the street," but "why is anyone choosing us at all, and what would make that choice easier or harder tomorrow."

## What the research surfaced

Field research with customers and employees produced two findings that shaped the rest of the work.

**A trust gap inside the brand promise.** Customers chose this chain because they saw it as a healthier alternative to other QSR options. But the same customers were conflicted: they suspected the food itself wasn't as high quality as the positioning suggested. The brand was being chosen on a promise the customer didn't fully believe. That's a fragile loyalty position. And no points program fixes it.

**Friction at the counter when language was a barrier.** Customers and employees both reported frustration with the ordering process when language or communication challenges were present. The interaction designed around speed and standardization broke down for anyone outside the assumed default. This was a service-level problem with operational consequences: slower service, wrong orders, embarrassed customers, frustrated staff.

The brief asked how to retain customers through a better loyalty program. The research surfaced two reasons customers were ambivalent about the brand in the first place: perceived quality, and friction at point of order. A loyalty program built on top of either of those problems would have been stacking incentives on a cracked foundation.

## The combined journey: <em>customer and employee on the same canvas.</em>

Back at the studio, I worked with the visual designer to build a combined customer-and-employee journey. Most journey maps stop at the customer's experience. Putting the employee on the same canvas, across front-of-house, register, and kitchen, made the friction at language barriers legible to the client as a service problem, not just a customer-experience problem. It also primed the workshop room to think about the service end-to-end, which mattered for what came next.

## The co-design workshop: <em>franchisees and the C-suite in one room.</em>

The workshop was deliberately mixed: CMO, CTO, members of their staffs, franchisees, and even restaurant workers, all in one room. That composition mattered. Strategy decisions about a QSR brand routinely happen far from the people who actually run the restaurants. Putting franchisees and workers in the same room as the C-suite changed which ideas were taken seriously and which assumptions held up under questioning.

We presented findings from the research, then ran facilitated exercises to define a vision for the company's future. I facilitated one of the breakout teams. By the end of the day, participants had generated more than 100 new ideas.

## The recap video, <em>an unusual opportunity.</em>

I had an unusual opportunity on this project: to learn a new craft and try a different way of presenting our work. I edited a recap video of the workshop day. The video told the story of the day: the energy in the room, the back-and-forth across the table, the way ideas built on each other. It made the value of the co-design portion of our process visible in a way a deck couldn't.

This mattered for two reasons. First, the client had people across the organization who hadn't been in the room and needed to understand what had happened. A video carried the energy of the day in a way that bullet-point summaries flatten. Second, it gave the workshop participants something to point to internally when they advocated for the ideas they'd generated. The artifact became a tool the client used after we left.

## Distillation: <em>from 100 to 40.</em>

After the workshop, the team distilled the raw ideas down to roughly 40 concepts. The work in that phase was less about generating and more about evaluating: for each idea, what made it useful against the goals the client had set? Which ideas addressed the trust gap? Which addressed the ordering friction? Which were operationally feasible inside a franchise model?

## Four things <em>I'm carrying forward.</em>

**The brief is a starting point, not a contract.** The most valuable move on this project was the team's willingness to push back on the brief itself. If we'd taken the loyalty-program framing at face value, we would have produced a competent points-system redesign and missed the actual problem. Senior service design work means being willing to argue with the question on the table, with evidence.

**Expand the competitive set until it tells you something true.** Treating "bringing lunch from home" as a competitor reframed the strategic conversation more than any single research finding. The frame you choose for the market shapes everything that follows.

**Put the people who run the service in the room with the people who set the strategy.** The workshop's mixed composition was load-bearing: CMO, CTO, franchisees, and workers in one room. Ideas got tested in real time against operational reality. That doesn't happen when strategy and operations meet only through slide decks.

**Find the unexpected deliverable.** The recap video wasn't in the original scope. Editing it together taught me a new craft and gave the client something they didn't know they needed. Looking for the artifact that doesn't exist yet, the one that carries the work into rooms you won't be in, is part of the job.
