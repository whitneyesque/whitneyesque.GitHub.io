## A pilot that grew faster than the process behind it

Allied Solutions runs RefundPlus, a service that calculates and processes refunds on cancelled GAP and other ancillary auto-loan products, the ones that pay out when a car is totaled, repossessed, or paid off early. It started as a small bet: a manual, hand-stitched pilot for a few lender clients. It worked. Then federal regulations changed. Lenders were now required to refund customers' money in every applicable case, on tight timelines. In some states, as little as thirty days. Demand for RefundPlus surged past what the manual process could absorb.

Allied came to Harmonic Design with a sharp brief: turn a bootstrapped pilot into a profitable, automated, scalable service, without breaking the version already running.

The complication was that no one inside Allied owned the whole picture. RefundPlus moves data and money across four kinds of actor: the lender who originated the loan, the dealer who sold the ancillary product, the provider who underwrites it, and Allied itself in the middle. Each one held part of the truth. None of them held all of it.

## What the service had grown into

Phase 1 was reconnaissance. The team ran inside-out workshops with Allied's operations, implementation, support, and sales teams to map the current state from the inside. Then they ran outside-in interviews with lenders and dealers to compare what Allied thought it was delivering with what its partners were actually receiving.

The two views did not match. Inside Allied, "the customer" generally meant the lender. Outside, the experience was shaped just as much by dealers (who fed product data into the system) and providers (who actually issued the refunds). The process Allied could see end-to-end from inside turned out to be only a portion of the service its partners were experiencing.

A second misalignment surfaced quickly. Inside the company, RefundPlus was treated as a product: something Allied had built and now sold. But it behaved like a service. The multi-party coordination and the dependency on partners outside Allied's walls weren't yet legible to the people running it.

## The turn: <em>dealers weren't the bottleneck</em>

Going in, the working hypothesis inside Allied was that dealers were where refund processing slowed down. The outside-in research said otherwise. Most processing delays traced to providers, the entities actually cutting refund checks. Dealers, meanwhile, contributed a different problem: many didn't know the refund regulations had legal teeth, or how short the timelines now were. Their lack of urgency was real, but it wasn't the root cause anyone had assumed.

<InsightCallout label="The reframe">
  The service had a *coordination problem*, not a dealer problem.
</InsightCallout>

That reframe shifted the design question from "how do we manage dealers" to "how do we make provider behavior, lender visibility, and dealer awareness work as one system." It also shifted what Allied needed to build: not a tighter process for one actor, but a connective layer that gave every actor the part of the picture they needed to act on time.

## Designing the future state with the people who'd run it

Phase 2 was collaborative. Rather than design the future state in private and present it, we built it with the people who would run it: operations, technology, partner management, and leadership inside Allied.

The workshops produced four artifacts that fit together:

- A future-state **service blueprint**, read in columns (moments) and rows (lender actions, touchpoints, frontstage Allied actions, backstage Allied actions, partner involvement, supporting processes/policies/technology, and a status milestone row that tracked how a refund's state should be represented through the entire flow).
- A **capability inventory**: a structured catalog of every capability Allied would need to operationalize the blueprint, tagged by business area and by feature concept, sized for project planning.
- Two views of an **evolution map**: the same set of capabilities organized once by business capability and once by feature concept. The point of two views is that program managers and product owners ask different questions of the same work, and the work shouldn't have to be re-organized to answer both.
- A set of **moment briefs**: closer-in views of specific high-stakes moments in the future service, written to support the workstreams that would carry implementation forward.

The blueprint did one thing on purpose that's easy to miss in a static deliverable. Wherever the workshop group could not resolve a design question (whether Allied should own a given capability, what data Allied actually needed from the lender at loan origination), the unresolved question stayed on the blueprint as an orange "big rock" sticky note, not smoothed over. The blueprint told the truth about what wasn't decided yet, and named who would need to decide it.

The recommended future state vision that emerged from this work was a more comprehensive automated system: real-time status visibility for lenders through a dashboard, a dealer portal, electronic payment options, and a consultative support layer for lenders working through complex cases. We weighed several alternative concepts and folded most into the recommended vision. A few stayed out of scope for what the workshop participants could carry forward at that moment.

## What it took to make a back-office service feel like a service

The harder, less visible part of this engagement was helping Allied see RefundPlus as a service in the first place: not a product they sold to lenders, but a coordinated set of behaviors across four kinds of partner, most of whom Allied did not employ.

That reframe didn't happen in a single moment. It happened in workshops where someone would describe a current-state problem and the room would walk back through the blueprint together and find the actual cause was a step earlier or later in the system. By the close of the engagement, the team's project retrospective named "the client started to think of RefundPlus as a service, not a product" as one of the engagement's outcomes. That framing shift doesn't show up on a roadmap, but it determines whether the future state can be built at all.

## What Happened Next

Allied accepted the future state vision and moved into the activation phase. The B2B-facing work — the lender dashboard, dealer portal, electronic payment options, and consultative support layer — was approved as the foundation for the next phase of build. The borrower-facing layer was deliberately scoped out as outside the immediate roadmap.

## What I'm Still Thinking About

Not every stakeholder accepted that this was service design's job. One Allied leader, in the team retrospective, took the position that service design shouldn't be used for strategy. The position is real, and it shows up at companies that haven't yet seen this kind of work pay out. The future state vision is the strongest response I have to that objection: a strategic deliverable produced through service design methods, scoped against the operational reality of the people who'd have to run it. The work answered the objection more credibly than any argument I could have made on its behalf.
