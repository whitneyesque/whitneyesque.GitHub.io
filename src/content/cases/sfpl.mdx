## A 146-year-old library, <em>asked to plan for a city it no longer fully recognized.</em>

The San Francisco Public Library has served the city since 1878. By the early 2020s it was operating in a San Francisco that had changed faster than its strategy could keep up with: shifting demographics, rising digital expectations, and a workforce of roughly 800 staff who knew the gap between policy and practice better than anyone.

SFPL's leadership wanted a strategic plan that wouldn't sit on a shelf. They assembled a consulting team (Gensler, Margaret Sullivan Studio, Harmonic Design, Contigo Communications, and Corey, Canapary & Galanis Research) to run what the firm later described as the most rigorous strategic planning process in public library history.

Harmonic Design's piece of that work was the part that decides whether a strategic plan ever becomes real: getting the people who deliver the service to shape the strategy that governs them.

## The real problem: <em>a strategic plan that staff would actually carry.</em>

Large public library systems share a familiar pattern. Leadership writes a plan, the plan gets adopted, and the people who actually deliver service every day either never see it or never recognize themselves in it. The plan becomes a document instead of a practice.

SFPL's leadership chose not to repeat that pattern. They committed to a process that would put frontline staff in the same conversations as executives and that would treat the library's culture as a thing to protect, not paper over.

## The ambassador model: <em>thirty-two staff inside the strategy.</em>

Thirty-two staff members, drawn from departments across the system, were chosen as ambassadors. They carried two responsibilities at once: bringing the texture of frontline work into strategic conversations, and bringing the strategic conversations back to colleagues who weren't in the room.

The ambassador model is the case's structural argument. It's also the most portable thing about the work. The library-specific findings stay local, but the shape of how an 800-person organization can include its frontline in its own strategy travels to any service-delivery institution.

## What the work produced: <em>a unanimous vote on Vision 2030.</em>

In 2024, the San Francisco Public Library Commission unanimously adopted the strategic plan, the **Vision 2030** plan, committing to a six-year effort to create "a democratic, equitable, and vibrant San Francisco for everyone." The plan named five strategic priorities.
