## A 145-year-old library system <em>needed a five-year plan its staff would actually carry.</em>

The San Francisco Public Library serves one of the most diverse cities in the United States from 28 branches, with a staff of between 501 and 1,000. In 2023, the library began its most ambitious strategic planning process to date, what would become Vision 2030. To build it, SFPL brought in a consortium led by Gensler, with Margaret Sullivan Studio, Harmonic Design, Contigo Communications, and Corey, Canapary & Galanis Research.

The risk in this kind of work is well known to anyone who has watched a strategic plan get adopted on Tuesday and ignored by Friday. A document, however well-written, does not change an organization. What changes an organization is whether the people who have to carry the plan recognize themselves in it.

Harmonic Design's job inside the consortium was to make sure that recognition got built in, not bolted on. That meant updating organizational values to match staff and community needs, and preparing the organization for the change ahead. The mechanism was a staff Ambassador Program: 32 people drawn from across the system, engaged across five workshops, running in parallel with a Management Team (MTeam) track that did the same work at a leadership altitude.

## The real problem: <em>a strategy is only as good as the people who carry it.</em>

SFPL's leadership did not need to be convinced that strategic planning mattered. They needed a process that produced a plan staff trusted, not one staff tolerated. That is a different problem, and it is the one service design is built for.

The risk for any organization at this scale is the gap between leadership vision and frontline reality. Branch staff know things the executive team can't see from the central office; the executive team sees pressures the branches don't carry. A strategic plan that doesn't close that gap doesn't get carried. It gets quietly worked around. The Ambassador Program was designed to close that gap on purpose.

## The mechanism: <em>32 staff, five sessions, two tracks running in parallel.</em>

The 32 ambassadors were selected to represent the full surface area of the organization: different branches, different departments, different levels of seniority, different lengths of tenure. Across five sessions over nine months, they did real work. They surfaced internal insights about what was and wasn't true on the ground. They drafted and pressure-tested organizational values. They modeled what those values would feel like in a moment of service, and identified and prioritized the opportunities the strategic plan needed to address.

In parallel, the Management Team (MTeam) ran its own track of sessions covering the same ground from leadership's vantage point. The two tracks were designed to feed each other. What ambassadors surfaced about frontline reality went up; what MTeam clarified about strategic intent came down. By the time the consortium drafted the plan, both groups had handled the material with their own hands.

## What the Ambassadors Actually Produced

The ambassadors didn't just give input. They drafted and pressure-tested the organization's new values. The five values that ended up in the published Vision 2030 plan — Well-being, Equity, Community, Collaboration, Exploration — went through ambassador hands before they reached the Library Commission. Specific phrasing carries the working language of the people who would have to live with it: *We strengthen our communities when we come together to engage, inspire, celebrate and learn from each other. We embrace diverse perspectives through thoughtful collaboration.*

Values written by leadership and handed down rarely survive contact with daily branch operations. Values written with the people doing branch operations come pre-tested.

## Designing for trust <em>as much as for output.</em>

By the second Ambassador session, we had a problem. A portion of the 32 ambassadors had arrived at the program visibly skeptical, and a vocal group was openly hostile to both library leadership and the consultant team. Some of that skepticism was specific: prior experience of consultants who had shown up, extracted input, and disappeared. Some of it was structural: a workforce with active labor concerns being asked to participate in a planning process its leadership had commissioned. All of it was reasonable. None of it was workable for the program as designed.

The internal diagnosis was blunt. The Gensler-prepared meeting record from May 17 captured it: staff felt the process was a "check the box" exercise, and the next session would have to reinforce that they were "playing a meaningful role" before any other work could happen. *Past is not a prologue* was the frame we had to earn.

What that meant in practice was [**FILL IN: ONE OR TWO SPECIFIC THINGS YOU CHANGED ABOUT THE 6/2 SESSION OR THE BROADER PROGRAM IN RESPONSE**]. Outside the workshops, it meant [**FILL IN: ONE OR TWO BEHIND-THE-SCENES MOVES BETWEEN SESSIONS**].

The agenda the team ran on June 2 looked similar to the agenda we had planned in April. The room was different.

<InsightCallout label="The principle">
Feedback wasn't collected. It was visibly metabolized, in front of the people who gave it.
</InsightCallout>

<PullQuote attribution="Staff ambassador, mid-series feedback">
I really appreciate seeing that the feedback from the last session was taken into account. Thank you!
</PullQuote>

That comment looks small on the page. In a room that started where this one started, it was the entire game. It meant a staff member who had arrived skeptical now trusted that the process was real. That trust is the precondition for them carrying the plan back into a branch and acting like it belongs to them.

## The artifacts <em>the work produced.</em>

The engagement generated a working library of artifacts that drove the synthesis: consolidated values profiles built from individual and small-group worksheets; a mapped poster set traced across teams so the lineage of every value could be seen; "moment cards" used to model values inside concrete service interactions; opportunity cards, prioritization posters, and strategic-alignment worksheets for the final session; and a shared collaboration board (the SFPL Collaboration Board) that held the cross-session through-line.

The first artifact in that sequence was the Ambassadors Team Purpose Statement, drafted from the kickoff worksheets through an affinity mapping exercise and written from the perspective of the Ambassador team rather than the consultants. That choice mattered. The document the ambassadors received back at session two was a statement of who *they* were and what *they* were there to do, in their own collective voice. It was the first signal that this process would treat their input as the source material, not as feedback to be filtered through a consultant's framing. Most strategic planning documents make the opposite choice by default. This one did not, and that set the precedent for everything that followed.

Listing them here isn't about output volume. It's to make visible the kind of decision the work required: how to take a value drafted in a Tuesday workshop, trace it to a frontline service moment, and then to a strategic priority the Library Commission could vote on. That traceability is the design problem inside this kind of engagement. It is what separates structured organizational design from facilitation alone.

## Outcome: the plan was adopted unanimously, <em>and the staff knew why.</em>

In December 2023, the San Francisco Public Library Commission adopted the Vision 2030 strategic plan unanimously, committing SFPL to a five-year program organized around five strategic priorities and the explicit goal of "a democratic, equitable, and vibrant San Francisco for everyone."

<PullQuote attribution="Michael Lambert, City Librarian, San Francisco Public Library">
This partnership between SFPL and our consulting team resulted in a strategic framework that's tailored to community needs while aligning with organizational values. It not only ensures SFPL's adaptability in an ever-developing culture but also serves as a perfect example for organizations navigating change and seeking to set a course for future excellence.
</PullQuote>

> "Creating a culture of experimentation that rewards creativity and failure as a learning moment is important. We need to foster a staff that is not afraid to take risks and learn from their experiences."

**— Commissioner interview, Vision 2030 process**

The unanimous adoption is the headline. The more interesting outcome is the one that doesn't show up in a vote count: by the time the plan reached the Commission, the staff who had to carry it had already handled its values, tested its priorities against their own working reality, and seen their feedback show up in the next session's materials. The plan didn't need to be sold internally because it had been built internally.

### And it kept working.

In March 2026, two and a half years after adoption, SFPL's Chief of Public Services presented the Vision 2030 midyear progress report to the Library Commission. Among the four pillars she named under "What's Ahead," alongside OKR targets, iteration and evolution, and service prioritization, was *Staff Ambassador Engagement*. The mechanism we built to land the plan in 2023 had, by 2026, become part of how the organization works. That is the harder outcome. Strategic plans get adopted all the time. The ones that get carried are the ones whose conditions hold.
